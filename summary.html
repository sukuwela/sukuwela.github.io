• My Portfolio Website

Summary and Incorporation of Findings

By Shanaya Ukuwela

User Interviews

    View User Interviews

User interviews are necessary in order to get an idea of what sort of audience you are trying to attract before creating your project. For my portfolio website, I am trying to use it as a "fancy" resume, highlighting my skills as well as my personality. In order to make sure that the content I provided would actually be what employers would want, I interviewed Professor Lumsdaine since I am interested in UX design. I wanted to know what I should focus on if I wanted to pursue a career in that field. I also interviewed classmates that would have a similar idea of what I should provide if I wanted to enter a career in Computer Science. Lastly, I also interviewed people unfamiliar with computer science, in case there would be, for example, a CEO unfamiliar with Computer Science that was in search for a freelance designer or programmer.

Competitor Analysis

    View Competitor Analysis

A competitor analysis is where you research other products that are trying to achieve the same goal as you with their services. In my case, I was trying to compare how I would fare against other "portfolio style" websites. So, I did a competitor analysis on three different websites: About.me, LinkedIn, and PortfolioBox - websites whose main goal is to help users attain a job. I analyzed these websites in particular because each had their own unique strong points: LinkedIn was heavily career-focused, About.me focused on simple content, and PortfolioBox focused on creativity and visual design. By analyzing these three sites, I was able to see how these features benefitted as well as hindered their goal.

Feature Value Matrix

    View Feature Value Matrix

Feature Value Matrices help to understand and define what are the key parts of your project. By doing a feature value matrix, I was able to pin-point the features that I absolutely needed for my website so that it would not only work well, but one would receive the content they wanted to see. I defined the features I needed were a profile photo and my name, an about section, professional skills section, navigation, personal section and design elements.

Feature Prioritization

    View Feature Prioritization

Feature Prioritization allows one to visualize your Feature Value Matrix so that you can further understand what features are most important for your product. By using the feature prioritization and kano model, it allowed me to really think about what was most important for my website. The feature value matrix rated what features are most beneficial, but the visual aspect of the feature prioritization allowed me to further narrow down what features I absolutely needed for my website. As such, I realized that I needed to focus on navigation and make sure my content was easily accessible and readable.

User Stories

    View User Stories

User Stories give you a deeper understanding of what kind of users you are expecting to use your product. In my case, my website is a portfolio, so I assumed, for the most part, what a hiring manager would want or expect from my website. I drew from my feature value matrix and created stories based off of those features, elaborating when necessary, and allowing me to further understand what a hiring manager wanted out of each feature, and how important it was to them. I also focused on making stories for my competitors, and tried to analyze what they would find from the various features of my website.

Cognitive Walkthrough

    View Cognitive Walkthrough

Cognitive Walkthroughs allow you to observe how your user would navigate your website, so that you can understand what features are actually beneficial to your site, and vice versa. I attempted three different cognitive walkthroughs, and focused on asking questions that about my site that I had a feeling were not as up-to-par as it should be. I asked them to find specific content on my site, and was able to see that the navigation bar titles could be misleading. I also asked about the overall design, as well as content of the site, to see what parts they liked and what parts seemed to bother them. I was able to see that I needed to make some changes in my navigation, as well as layout of my website. Most importantly, doing a cognitive walkthrough allowed me to really understand the user's thought process, because I could directly ask them what they would find confusing or difficult throughout the walkthrough.

User Test

    View User Test 1 View User Test 2

User testing allows you to test specific parts of your product so that you can improve on it if any problems seem to occur. I find that for my website, user testing is effective in getting quantitative results on specific details of your website. I used Usability Hub to create two User tests, one to analyze the navigation to a specific piece of content on my site, and another to analyze the overall "look" of my site. I found that I was able to see what the majority of people attempted to do on my site, and realized how much these specific features were causing problems for my site. For example, I found that many people could not pin-point where to find my skills, and people found the "fun stuff" section difficult to understand.

Summary of Findings

Overall, researching and testing really helped me understand how to create my website. What I found most helpful was the user research. This is my first attempt at building a website, so I had no idea what exactly I was trying to accomplish. Researching and, most especially, figuring out the key features of my website really helped me begin the coding process. If I had simply jumped straight in to coding the website, the end result would have been a mess. Through this research, I realized that the most important part of my website was the content, and following that, being able to easily access my content. As such, I didn't pay much attention to the overall design of my website, and went for a very clean, very minimal look. Though it wasn't my intention to create a very Tumblr-esque, "blog"-looking website, but this was my first time building a website. All I knew was that you should be able to access and see my content. By doing so, I realized how important of a role spacing and font played in the design of my website. You don't have to add bunch of colors and fancy animations to make something visually pleasing. With almost little- to-no CSS, a website can still be pretty.

Delving into the user testing part of the project allowed me to really see the results of my work. By implementing cognitive walkthroughs, I was able to get inside the mind of a typical user, and observe how they would use my website. I hadn't realized how confusing it was to find the "skills" section of my website. After going through cognitive walkthroughs as well user testing, many people seemed to just have educated guesses as to where it could be. I realized that having tabs like "about", "education", and "projects" made it difficult to pin-point where you'd learn about my personal skills. Another thing I noticed was that people weren't exactly sure what my website was for. The fact that when you initially open up my website, and all you see is my face and name, was not enough to really understand what the point of this site was, or rather, make people continue to use it. Some people mentioned that the website didn't really appear to be like a portfolio website for a computer programmer, but rather a designer, which I found surprising to hear.

In summary, gathering all of this information has helped not only create a website but also given me a clear idea of what needs to be improved on.

Incorporation of Findings

After conducting all of these user interviews and tests, I realize that I will need to make some minor adjustments to the navigation, design and even content of my website. First off, I think a very key part of my website is being able to find my skills. I feel like many employers would look to find your skills first more than anything, so that they can confirm that you are capable for the job. However, since many people had trouble finding where my skills would be, I think I will include a tab, or even add to the title of one of my sections to make it more clear. Instead of having an education tab, I will rewrite it and have an "Educations and Skills" tab. In my head, I feel that the skills should be paired with my education, because that is where I have learned the bulk of my programming languages. In terms of design, I think I will alter my website so that when you first open it, you are able to see the about section, or just a simple sentence describing who I am and what I do. I think I will write down my occupation below my name, so that people will understand who I am and what I do. Lastly, I think I will alter the about section of my site, since people found that it was vague and didn't really encompass what exactly my goal was with this website.

To be honest, due to the fact that my website's design is so minimal, making these changes was actually quite simple. Incorporating these changes took little to no time at all, but has allowed my website to be a little bit more user-friendly. However, I feel that another reason improving my website was simple was because I did the user research in order to understand what my website would be. I was able to prevent problems that would have arose if I did not research. It's possible that my layout would have been un-usable, and the content that my users wanted would not be there. Also, I realize that due to the fact that my website is completely navigational via scrolling, it made it a lot easier for the user to find things as well - you didn't have to click random buttons to find the information you wanted. However, though my website has been improved, I still have personal improvements I would like to do.

All in all, incorporating these user issues was a seamless process, but personally, I would like to put a bit more effort into the design of my website, since that was never my main focus throughout its creation. Now that the content has been established, I feel that I can now play around with the "look" of my website, and try and attempt a more colorful, less minimal sort of style in the future.
